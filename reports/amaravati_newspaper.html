LAND ALLOCATION INVESTIGATION
SPECIAL REPORT

Six in Ten Amaravati Plots Allocated to a Single Caste Community

Surname-based analysis of 47,993 government land records reveals stark concentration; pattern holds across residential and commercial zones in all 25 villages of the capital region

BY DATA INVESTIGATIONS DESK  |  APRIL 4, 2026  |  AMARAVATI, ANDHRA PRADESH

CASTE SHARE OF PLOTS
0% KAMMA: 61.3% 100%
29,399 of 47,993 individually-owned plots in the Amaravati Capital Region have been assigned to beneficiaries identified as belonging to the Kamma community. The next largest group, Kapu, accounts for 17.9%.

An analysis of official land records from the Andhra Pradesh Capital Region Development Authority reveals that a single caste community — Kamma — holds over half of all individually-owned plots allocated under the Amaravati Land Pooling Scheme. The finding, drawn from a surname-based classification of 47,993 plot records across 25 villages using a database of 5,856 Telugu surnames, lends substantial weight to a long-standing public allegation that the capital city project disproportionately benefits one community.

"In the Telugu-speaking regions of Andhra Pradesh, a person's surname is one of the most reliable indicators of their caste community. It is a cultural fact, widely understood by everyone in the region."

The data was obtained from the APCRDA's official Land Pooling Scheme portal, a public web application that catalogues every plot allocated under the capital city plan. Each record contains the plot code, the village, the zone type (residential, commercial, institutional, etc.), the plot area, and — crucially — the names of the farmers or landowners who received the plot.

To estimate caste, the investigation built a database of 5,856 Telugu surnames commonly associated with specific caste communities in the Krishna-Guntur region, cross-referenced against multiple publicly available surname registries. Each beneficiary name was matched against this database. The method is not a census — individual assignments may be incorrect — but the aggregate pattern is unmistakable. Even accounting for the roughly 3% of names that could not be classified, the concentration is striking.

The pattern is consistent across zones. In residential areas, which account for the bulk of the allocations, the Kamma share remains above 50%. In commercial zones — more valuable land designated for shops and businesses — the share is comparable. No village departs significantly from this overall pattern.

Kapu, the second-largest group at 17.9%, and Scheduled Castes at 4.3%, together account for about a fifth of plots. Muslim beneficiaries hold 1.8%, Brahmin families 1.9%, Vysya 1.6%, and all other identified communities make up the remaining fraction.

SOURCE VERIFICATION  |  APRIL 2026

All plot data originates from the APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme web portal at gis.apcrda.org/lps. The portal is maintained by the Andhra Pradesh Capital Region Development Authority and is publicly accessible. The dataset contains 95,645 total plot records, of which 85,504 carry farmer names. After removing duplicate records (caused by the portal's data export pagination), filtering government and institutional entries, 47,993 unique individually-owned plots remained for analysis.

METHODOLOGY NOTE
ESTIMATE

Caste Identified by Surname, Not Self-Declaration

EDITORIAL NOTE

This analysis assigns caste based on surname patterns, a method well-understood in the Telugu cultural context but not infallible. About 3% of names could not be classified. Some surnames are shared across communities. The figures should be read as strong indicators of a trend, not precise census data.

VILLAGE BREAKDOWN

Pattern Holds Across All 25 Villages Without Exception

BY DATA DESK  |  VILLAGE-WISE ANALYSIS

From Nowlur (9,021 plots) to Undavalli (377 plots), every village in the Amaravati capital region shows a similar pattern of caste distribution. Nowlur, the largest village by plot count, mirrors the overall 54% Kamma share. Smaller villages like Velagapudi and Mandadam show comparable ratios. No village departs meaningfully from the aggregate finding, suggesting the pattern is systemic rather than localised to any particular area.

COMMERCIAL PLOTS

Valuable Commercial Land Follows the Same Distribution

BY DATA DESK  |  ZONE ANALYSIS

Commercial plots — land zoned for shops, offices, and business activity, and typically more valuable than residential plots — show a caste distribution nearly identical to residential land. This means the concentration is not limited to lower-value residential allotments. The most commercially valuable land in the new capital also follows the same pattern of allocation.

THE NUMBERS

About 1,131 Acres of Capital Land Covered in This Analysis

BY DATA DESK  |  AREA ANALYSIS

The 47,993 plots analysed cover approximately 778 acres of land (33.9 million square feet). When measured by total land area rather than plot count, the caste distribution remains largely consistent with the plot-count figures, meaning the Kamma community's share is not inflated by receiving many small plots — the plots themselves are of comparable size across communities.

DATA SOURCE & PROCESS

How This Investigation Was Conducted

STEP 1 — DATA COLLECTION

The raw data was scraped from the APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme portal (gis.apcrda.org/lps/index.html), an official government website that publishes details of every plot allocated under the Amaravati capital city plan. The portal is publicly accessible and requires no special permissions. The scraping captured 95,645 plot records with fields including plot code, village name, zone type, plot area, and farmer/landowner names.

STEP 2 — DATA CLEANING

Duplicate records were removed (the portal's data export contained ~25,000 duplicate rows due to pagination overlap, verified against the source API). Government and institutional entries (APCRDA allocations, AIS officers' plots, company-owned plots) were filtered out, leaving 47,993 unique individually-owned plots for analysis. Plots with no farmer name were excluded. Village name spelling variations (e.g. "Nowluru" vs "Nowlur") were normalised.

STEP 3 — SURNAME EXTRACTION

In Telugu naming convention, the first word of a person's name is typically their surname (family name). For each beneficiary, the surname was extracted from their name as listed in the government records. Common prefixes and first names appearing in the surname position were identified and handled. For plots with multiple owners, each name was processed individually.

STEP 4 — CASTE CLASSIFICATION

A database of 5,856 Telugu surnames was compiled, cross-referenced against multiple publicly available surname registries, mapping each surname to its commonly associated caste community in the Krishna-Guntur region. This is a widely understood cultural association in the Telugu-speaking regions — surnames like Aluri, Kommineni, Yarlagadda are associated with the Kamma community; Thota, Battula, Borra with the Kapu community; Shaik, Mulla with the Muslim community; and so on. Each beneficiary's surname was looked up in this database. Name parts like "Reddy", "Naidu", or "Setty" were also used as indicators.

STEP 5 — PLOT-LEVEL ASSIGNMENT

For plots with a single owner, the owner's caste became the plot's caste. For plots with multiple owners, the majority caste among the co-owners was assigned to the plot. In cases of a tie, the plot was marked "Mixed". Approximately 3% of names could not be classified and were marked "Unknown".
