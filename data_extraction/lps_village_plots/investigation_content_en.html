Nearly 6 in 10 Amaravati Plots Went to One Caste Community

Analysis of 47,993 government land records reveals that the Kamma community received 57.4% of all individually-owned plots allocated under the Land Pooling Scheme — more than the next six communities combined

BY DATA INVESTIGATIONS DESK  |  APRIL 2026  |  AMARAVATI, ANDHRA PRADESH

SHARE OF PLOTS BY CASTE

0% KAMMA: 57.4% 100%

27,555 of 47,993 plots went to beneficiaries identified as Kamma. The next largest — Kapu (13.0%) and Reddy (5.0%) — together account for less than a third of what Kamma holds alone.

An analysis of official land records from the Andhra Pradesh Capital Region Development Authority reveals that a single caste community — Kamma — holds nearly six in ten individually-owned plots allocated under the Amaravati Land Pooling Scheme. Out of 47,993 plots spread across 26 villages in the capital region, 27,555 were assigned to beneficiaries identified as belonging to the Kamma community — a 57.4% share that exceeds the next six largest communities combined.

"In the Telugu-speaking regions of Andhra Pradesh, a person's surname is one of the most reliable indicators of their caste community. It is a cultural fact, widely understood by everyone in the region."

The concentration is not confined to one type of land. In residential zones, which make up the bulk of the allocations, the Kamma share holds steady above 55%. In commercial zones — more valuable land designated for shops and businesses — the ratio is comparable. No single village among the 26 analysed departs meaningfully from this overall pattern, suggesting the distribution is systemic rather than localised.

After Kamma, the next largest groups are Kapu at 13.0% (6,260 plots), Reddy at 5.0% (2,398 plots), Brahmin at 4.4% (2,116 plots), and Vysya at 2.9% (1,415 plots). The analysis also identified previously invisible communities: Padmasali (2.4%), Mudiraj (0.9%), Gouda (0.5%), and Settibalija (0.3%). Scheduled Castes — broken into Mala (0.5%) and Madiga (0.1%) — account for about 1.5% combined. Muslim at 1.9%, Christian at 1.2%, and all other identified communities make up the remaining fraction.

The findings lend substantial weight to a long-standing public allegation that the Amaravati capital city project disproportionately benefits one community. The data is drawn from the APCRDA's own public portal and the caste assignment method, while an estimate rather than a census, produces a pattern too consistent across geography and zone type to be explained by chance.

CONTEXT

Why Does This Matter?

The Amaravati capital project displaced farmers from 26 villages under a "land pooling" model — farmers surrendered agricultural land and received developed plots in return. If one community received a disproportionate share, it raises questions about whether the scheme's benefits were distributed equitably across the region's diverse population.

GEOGRAPHY

Pattern Holds Across All 26 Villages

From Nowlur (the largest village by plot count) to Undavalli (the smallest), every village shows a similar caste distribution. The pattern is systemic, not localised to any particular area.

AREA

778 Acres of Capital Land Analysed

When measured by total land area rather than plot count, the distribution remains consistent — the Kamma community's share is not inflated by receiving many small plots.

COMMERCIAL LAND

Valuable Commercial Plots Follow the Same Pattern

Commercial plots — land zoned for shops, offices, and businesses, typically more valuable than residential plots — show a caste distribution nearly identical to residential land. The concentration is not limited to lower-value allotments; the most commercially valuable land in the new capital also follows the same pattern of allocation.

SMALLER COMMUNITIES

Marginalised Groups Hold a Fraction of Capital Land

Scheduled Castes (including Mala and Madiga sub-communities) account for about 1.5% of plots. Scheduled Tribes hold just 0.3%. Muslim beneficiaries received 1.9%, and Christians 1.2%. Together, these historically disadvantaged communities hold a small fraction of what the single largest group received.

DATA NOTE
ESTIMATE

Caste Identified by Surname, Not Self-Declaration

Caste was inferred using AI-assisted surname classification, not self-reported identity. The method is an estimate, not a census — individual entries may be misclassified. But the aggregate pattern is consistent across every dimension of the data. Full methodology is in the Data Source & Process tab.
