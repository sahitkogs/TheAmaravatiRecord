AI Disclosure

Where this publication uses machine-learning systems, where it does not, and how model outputs are verified before they become journalism.

LAST UPDATED: APRIL 10, 2026  |  VERSION 1.0

In 2026 it is no longer interesting to ask whether an independent newsroom uses large language models. The interesting questions are: where, for what, how the outputs are verified, and whether the human writing the story could replace the model without the story suffering. This page answers those questions for The Amaravati Record in concrete terms. If you find it insufficient, write to the editor and it will be expanded.

Systems used

Google Gemini 2.5 Flash

Role: Per-name caste classification for the Amaravati Land Pooling Scheme beneficiary roll.

How it is used: The scraped beneficiary roll contains approximately 48,000 unique farmer names. A deterministic surname-corpus lookup (5,500+ surnames hand-curated from 19 sources) classifies the large majority of these cases. For residual cases where the surname is ambiguous, missing from the corpus, or where the "surname" field actually contains a first name, the name is passed to Gemini 2.5 Flash with a structured prompt asking for (a) the caste classification, (b) a confidence level, and (c) a brief reason. The classifier is run in batches with controlled temperature and an explicit disagreement-reporting instruction.

How the output is verified:

  • Model outputs are cross-checked against the hand-curated corpus for the overlapping cases. Disagreement rates are measured and published on the dashboard's methodology tab.
  • A sampled random subset of model outputs is manually reviewed before each major report is published.
  • The full model output is retained in version control so that any reader can re-run the classifier against the same inputs and verify reproducibility.
  • Aggregate statistics are reported with explicit uncertainty bounds derived from the measured disagreement rate.

Why we use it: The beneficiary roll is large enough that manual classification of every name would take months. A deterministic lookup alone leaves gaps of several thousand names. The model acts as a force-multiplier for the human corpus, and the two checking each other is more reliable than either alone.

What we do not use it for: We do not use Gemini or any other model to write prose, compose headlines, generate quotes, draft analysis, or make editorial judgments about which findings to publish. We also do not use it to generate or modify images.

No other ML systems are in the production pipeline

Specifically: this publication does not use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini Pro, Llama, or any other model to write articles, ledes, headlines, pull quotes, or methodology text. It does not use AI image generation for masthead, illustration, or any editorial imagery. It does not use AI video or audio generation for any published content. It does not use automated summarization of source documents in a way that bypasses human reading.

Assistant usage (writing and editing)

The publisher occasionally uses general-purpose assistants (including Claude) as a drafting and editing aid: brainstorming lede phrasings, catching grammar and punctuation errors, suggesting structural improvements, generating HTML scaffolds, and debugging scripts. This is analogous to a spell-checker or a thesaurus and is not disclosed on a per-sentence basis.

The following editorial threshold applies: if a passage's core reasoning, investigation, or analysis was performed principally by a model rather than by the publisher, the report will be labelled with a prominent disclosure at the top stating so and explaining the model's contribution. To date, no such report has been published. Every investigation published here reflects reasoning performed and verified by the publisher personally, whatever drafting tools were used along the way.

Training data and model behavior

Readers should know that the classifier's output is sensitive to the training data of the underlying model. Gemini's training corpus skews towards the English-language public web, which carries its own biases about which surnames are associated with which communities. The hand-curated corpus we cross-check against was built from nineteen independent sources, several of which are regional Telugu-language community websites — a deliberate counterbalance to the web-general bias of the model. Where the two disagree, the disagreement is flagged and the entry falls into a "uncertain" bucket, not silently resolved.

The publication explicitly rejects the framing that the model is "the classifier" and the corpus is merely a "sanity check". Both are the classifier. The per-name attribution is the union of both, and the uncertainty bound is the measure of their disagreement.

Limits of the classification

We restate here what the reports themselves state at the top of every methodology page:

  • Caste classification based on name alone is an approximation, not a census.
  • Individual attributions may be wrong at the margins, especially for names drawn from regionally unusual surnames or names where the field contains only a first name.
  • Aggregate statistics (at the community, village, and plot-category level) are robust because individual-level errors average out. The reports quantify and disclose the residual error rates.
  • A reader who wants to contest an individual attribution can do so via the corrections process.

Data protection for model prompts

Names sent to Gemini for classification are sent over HTTPS to Google's API endpoints. Google's terms for the paid Gemini API state that paid-tier customer data is not used to train Google's models. The publisher uses the paid tier specifically for this reason. The raw beneficiary names are publicly available from the APCRDA portal, so no new privacy exposure is created by the classification process itself. The masked versions of names (with given names and plot identifiers redacted) are what appear in the published reports; the classifier operates on the unmasked private copy.

When this disclosure will be updated

This page is updated whenever a new model is added to the production pipeline, an existing model is replaced, or the editorial threshold for per-report disclosure is crossed. The "last updated" timestamp at the top of the page reflects the current version. Substantive changes are also noted in the corrections ledger.
