ABOUT THIS PUBLICATION

A One-Desk Newsroom for a City Still Being Drawn

Independent reporting on the making of a capital.

SAHIT KOGANTI  |  FOUNDER & EDITOR  |  APRIL 2026

The Amaravati Record is an independent newspaper organisation covering the construction, politics, and underlying data of Andhra Pradesh's capital city. It was founded in 2026 as a single-desk operation with a simple working principle: the capital is still being drawn, and the public deserves a record of how it is being drawn — who owns the land, which sites are actually rising out of the ground, and what the public rolls say once every line is read.

This publication does two kinds of work. The first is live — a tracker of construction and infrastructure sites across the Krishna–Guntur corridor, updated with real news and video feeds as those sites move. The second is slow — record-level investigations built from government datasets, cleaned, classified, and released with their pipelines intact so that any reader can check the arithmetic.

Our Motto

“Independent reporting on the making of a capital.”

Independence here means three things. First, the publication takes no party line — neither for nor against the capital project. Second, every finding is tied to a public source a reader can verify. Third, the publication is reader-supported and carries no advertising.

What We Cover

  • Land & pooling. The APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme, beneficiary rolls, and the demographic composition of allocation across the twenty-six capital villages.
  • Construction & infrastructure. A live map of twenty tracked sites, from government complexes to bridges, with real news and video dispatches for each one.
  • Data desk. Scraping, cleaning, and classification of public records — with methodology notes and open pipelines.
  • Editorial policy. Personally identifying information is masked in every public report; aggregate statistics remain untouched.

Methodology

Every investigation begins with a public data source — a government portal, a public roll, a ministry dataset. The data is scraped with a documented scraper, deduplicated against known collision patterns, and classified using a combination of hand-curated corpora and large-language-model assistance where the corpora run out. The full pipeline is released along with the report, and readers are invited to re-run it against their own sources.

The publication treats language models as collaborators, not oracles. Every model-assisted classification is cross-checked against a ground-truth corpus drawn from independent sources, and disagreement rates are reported openly.

Who Runs This

The Amaravati Record is edited by Sahit Koganti, an independent data journalist covering the capital region. This is a one-desk operation. There is no board, no advertiser, and no political affiliation.
