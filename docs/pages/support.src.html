Share Is The Currency

The Amaravati Record takes no donations, runs no ads, and asks nothing from its readers except attention. The only support this publication needs is the oldest kind — one reader telling another.

Most independent publications end their About page by asking you for money. This one does not. The cost of running The Amaravati Record is real but small, and the publisher has chosen to carry it as the cost of doing public-interest work rather than to build a donation infrastructure that would compete with the reporting for attention. What this publication needs instead is the thing that donation dollars are usually spent trying to manufacture: reach. If you want to support The Amaravati Record, share it. Every reader you bring is worth more than any amount you could send.

Six ways to share

1. Link to a specific report

If you run a blog, a newsletter, an academic page, a Substack, a GitHub README, or anything else with a URL, link directly to the report you found useful rather than to the home page. Deep links are worth many times more than a generic "check out this site" link. For the current investigation, the durable URL is:

https://sahitkogs.github.io/TheAmaravatiRecord/reports/lps-caste-dashboard.html

2. Send a report to someone who would care

Think of three specific people who would find a given report relevant — a researcher studying Indian land reform, a journalist at a mainstream outlet who might expand on it, a civil servant with context on the Amaravati capital programme, a friend who cares about caste politics, a professor who might assign it. Send it to them directly. A one-to-one forward from a trusted sender is the single most valuable distribution event for a small publication.

3. Post it on social media — with context

If you use X, Bluesky, Mastodon, LinkedIn, Threads, Reddit, or any of the Indian-language platforms, sharing a report with a sentence of your own context travels much further than a bare link. Say why you found it useful, or which finding surprised you, or which part you disagree with. Honest engagement is better advertising than praise.

If you want a pull-quote or a key statistic to anchor your post, feel free to screenshot any chart or pull-quote from a report. Everything on this site is CC BY 4.0; you do not need permission, just attribution.

4. Cite us in academic work

If you are writing a paper, thesis, book chapter, or conference submission and our work is relevant, please cite us. Academic citations are how independent data journalism builds long-term credibility and how findings survive past the news cycle. Our preferred citation format (APA style):

Koganti, S. (2026). [Report title]. The Amaravati Record. Retrieved from https://sahitkogs.github.io/TheAmaravatiRecord/

If you need a different citation style (Chicago, MLA, ACM, IEEE, or a specific journal format), the elements are: Author = Sahit Koganti; Publisher = The Amaravati Record; Year = 2026; URL = the specific report URL. Email the editor if you want a DOI; we will arrange one via Zenodo on request.

5. Republish under CC BY 4.0

If you run a publication and want to republish one of our reports in full — in English, or translated into Telugu, Tamil, Hindi, Kannada, Malayalam, Bengali, Marathi, or any other language — you do not need to ask permission. Our content is licensed under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0. Just credit us and link back to the original.

You can also syndicate partial excerpts with your own commentary, quote pull-out boxes, translate and publish on your own site, or even build a completely derivative analysis on top of our findings. All of that is pre-authorised. The only thing we ask is: if you do something substantial with a report, email the editor so we can link to your work from ours. That is the cycle that keeps the ecosystem healthy.

6. Translate a report

The single highest-leverage thing a multilingual reader can do for this publication is to translate a report into a language it does not yet exist in. A Telugu translation of the caste dashboard would reach an audience roughly a thousand times larger than the English version ever will. The licence already permits it; we will happily link to any translation you host anywhere, credit you as the translator, and mirror a copy here with your permission.

If you have translation skills and want a specific report to work on, email the editor. We can coordinate so the same report is not translated in parallel by several people.

Other non-financial ways to help

Send tips and documents

If you know something about Amaravati's land, construction, or governance that the public should know — even a fragment, a rumour, a pointer to a public record we might have missed — send it. Encrypted and anonymous channels are documented on the Contact page. Tips are worth more than donations; we cannot buy information.

Report a correction

Every factual error you catch and report makes every future reader of that report better informed. Corrections are logged in the public Corrections Ledger with credit to the reporter (or anonymously if you prefer). This is genuine support, not a favour; please do it.

Contribute to the surname corpus

The caste classification pipeline rests on a hand-curated surname directory. If you know a surname our directory is missing, misclassifying, or unfair to, email the editor with your correction and — ideally — a URL-backed source. The corpus is released under ODbL and every contribution improves the classifier for everyone.

Improve the code

The scrapers, classifiers, and build scripts are MIT-licensed and live in the public repository at github.com/sahitkogs/TheAmaravatiRecord. Pull requests, bug reports, and architectural suggestions are welcome. If you fix a bug or add a feature that makes the pipeline more reliable, you become part of the publication's technical infrastructure — a form of support that compounds over every future report.

Introduce us

If you know a journalist at a mainstream outlet, a professor who studies related topics, a data-journalism organisation, a librarian, a fellowship programme, or anyone else who would find this work useful, please make the introduction. A warm introduction from someone they trust is the most effective distribution channel there is.

Academic and research partnerships

If you are a university researcher studying Indian governance, land reform, caste representation, or data-journalism methods, The Amaravati Record welcomes collaboration. Datasets are free to use under ODbL 1.0; the publisher is available for co-authorship discussions, guest lectures, student thesis supervision, and joint methodology papers — all without fees. Email the editor with the subject line Collaboration: to start a conversation.

What this publication does not ask for

  • Money. No donations, no recurring support, no memberships, no tip jars, no Patreon, no Substack paid tier, no Ko-fi, no cryptocurrency wallets, no UPI links. If any website anywhere claims to accept donations on behalf of The Amaravati Record, it is fraudulent and should be reported.
  • Email signups. We do not run a newsletter, a mailing list, or a subscriber list. If you want to follow new reports, bookmark the Reports index or the repository and check it periodically. If a newsletter ever becomes appropriate, it will be announced here first.
  • Account creation. This site has no accounts, no login, no profile. There is nothing to sign up for.
  • Social-media follows. We do not currently maintain official social-media accounts. Any account claiming to represent The Amaravati Record on any platform is unauthorised until announced on this page.

The one thing worth remembering

Independent journalism survives or fails on word of mouth. The best moment to support this publication is the moment you finish reading a report that taught you something. Forward it, link it, quote it, translate it, or just tell one person about it. That is worth more than any amount of money you could have sent.
