LAND ALLOCATION INVESTIGATION
SPECIAL REPORT

Nearly 6 in 10 Amaravati Plots Allocated to a Single Caste Community

AI-assisted analysis of 47,993 government land records using 15,056 surname references and Gemini classification reveals stark concentration; pattern holds across residential and commercial zones in all 26 villages of the capital region

BY DATA INVESTIGATIONS DESK  |  APRIL 8, 2026  |  AMARAVATI, ANDHRA PRADESH

CASTE SHARE OF PLOTS
0% KAMMA: 57.4% 100%
27,555 of 47,993 individually-owned plots in the Amaravati Capital Region have been assigned to beneficiaries identified as belonging to the Kamma community. The next largest groups are Kapu (13.0%) and Reddy (5.0%).

An analysis of official land records from the Andhra Pradesh Capital Region Development Authority reveals that a single caste community — Kamma — holds nearly six in ten individually-owned plots allocated under the Amaravati Land Pooling Scheme. The finding, drawn from an AI-assisted classification of 47,993 plot records across 26 villages — using a reference database of 15,056 Telugu surnames and Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash model for per-name classification — lends substantial weight to a long-standing public allegation that the capital city project disproportionately benefits one community.

"In the Telugu-speaking regions of Andhra Pradesh, a person's surname is one of the most reliable indicators of their caste community. It is a cultural fact, widely understood by everyone in the region."

The data was obtained from the APCRDA's official Land Pooling Scheme portal, a public web application that catalogues every plot allocated under the capital city plan. Each record contains the plot code, the village, the zone type (residential, commercial, institutional, etc.), the plot area, and — crucially — the names of the farmers or landowners who received the plot.

To estimate caste, the investigation compiled a reference database of 15,056 Telugu surnames from 19 community and election data sources, then classified all 30,444 unique beneficiary names using Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash AI model with thinking enabled. The model was given each name, its village, and any surname references as supporting context, then asked to identify the most specific caste community possible. Over 99% of names were successfully classified across 50+ caste categories. The method is not a census — individual assignments may be incorrect — but the aggregate pattern is unmistakable.

The pattern is consistent across zones. In residential areas, which account for the bulk of the allocations, the Kamma share remains above 55%. In commercial zones — more valuable land designated for shops and businesses — the share is comparable. No village departs significantly from this overall pattern.

Kapu, the second-largest group at 13.0%, followed by Reddy at 5.0%, Brahmin at 4.4%, and Vysya at 2.9%. The analysis also identified previously invisible communities: Padmasali (2.4%), Mudiraj (0.9%), Gouda (0.5%), and Settibalija (0.3%). Scheduled Castes, now broken into Mala (0.5%) and Madiga (0.1%), account for about 1.5% combined. Muslim at 1.9%, Christian at 1.2%, and all other identified communities make up the remaining fraction.

VILLAGE BREAKDOWN

Pattern Holds Across All 25 Villages Without Exception

BY DATA DESK  |  VILLAGE-WISE ANALYSIS

From Nowlur (9,021 plots) to Undavalli (377 plots), every village in the Amaravati capital region shows a similar pattern of caste distribution. Nowlur, the largest village by plot count, mirrors the overall Kamma-dominant pattern. Smaller villages like Velagapudi and Mandadam show comparable ratios. No village departs meaningfully from the aggregate finding, suggesting the pattern is systemic rather than localised to any particular area.

COMMERCIAL PLOTS

Valuable Commercial Land Follows the Same Distribution

BY DATA DESK  |  ZONE ANALYSIS

Commercial plots — land zoned for shops, offices, and business activity, and typically more valuable than residential plots — show a caste distribution nearly identical to residential land. This means the concentration is not limited to lower-value residential allotments. The most commercially valuable land in the new capital also follows the same pattern of allocation.

DATA SOURCE & PROCESS

How This Investigation Was Conducted

STEP 1 — DATA COLLECTION

The raw data was scraped from the APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme portal (gis.apcrda.org/lps/index.html), an official government website that publishes details of every plot allocated under the Amaravati capital city plan. The portal is publicly accessible and requires no special permissions. The scraping captured 95,645 plot records with fields including plot code, village name, zone type, plot area, and farmer/landowner names.

STEP 2 — DATA CLEANING

Duplicate records were removed (the portal's data export contained ~25,000 duplicate rows due to pagination overlap, verified against the source API). Government and institutional entries (APCRDA allocations, AIS officers' plots, company-owned plots) were filtered out, leaving 47,993 unique individually-owned plots for analysis. Plots with no farmer name were excluded. Village name spelling variations (e.g. "Nowluru" vs "Nowlur") were normalised.

STEP 3 — SURNAME EXTRACTION

In Telugu naming convention, the first word of a person's name is typically their surname (family name). For each beneficiary, the surname was extracted from their name as listed in the government records. Common prefixes and first names appearing in the surname position were identified and handled. For plots with multiple owners, each name was processed individually.

STEP 4 — CASTE CLASSIFICATION

A reference database of 15,056 Telugu surnames was compiled from 19 community and election data sources, covering 33 caste categories. Each of the 30,444 unique beneficiary names was then classified using Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash AI model. The model was given the full name, village, and any matching surname references as supporting context — not as authoritative truth — and asked to identify the most specific caste community. Thinking mode was enabled so the model could reason through ambiguous cases. Over 99% of names were classified into 50+ specific caste communities, including previously invisible groups like Padmasali, Mudiraj, and Gouda.

STEP 5 — PLOT-LEVEL ASSIGNMENT

For plots with a single owner, the owner's caste became the plot's caste. For plots with multiple owners, the majority caste among the co-owners was assigned to the plot. In cases of a tie, the plot was marked "Mixed". Less than 1% of names could not be classified and were marked "Unknown".
