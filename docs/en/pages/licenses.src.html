REUSE & ATTRIBUTION

Licenses

Everything on this site is openly licensed. Different kinds of work carry different licences — here is the stack, in plain English, with the attribution format you need.

Andhra Record publishes three kinds of asset — prose, data, and code — and each carries a different open licence. This is the industry-standard stack for data journalism, and it mirrors what OpenStreetMap, Our World in Data, ProPublica, The Markup, and Bellingcat all use. You do not need to ask permission to reuse any of it; you just need to credit.

The three layers

● CONTENT
CC BY 4.0
Articles, reports, investigations, editorial commentary, and all written prose on this site. Reuse, translate, adapt, republish — even commercially — as long as you credit Andhra Record and link back.
Full text: creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0
● DATA
ODbL 1.0
Cleaned datasets, classification outputs, the surname directory, and database contents of interactive dashboards. Reuse freely, even commercially. If you publish a derivative database, release it under ODbL too.
Full text: opendatacommons.org/licenses/odbl/1-0
● CODE
MIT
Source code: scrapers, classifiers, build scripts, site JavaScript, CSS, and all pipeline tooling. Do whatever you want; keep the copyright notice. No warranty.
Full text: opensource.org/licenses/MIT

Which licence applies to what I want to reuse?

  • Reading a report and writing your own article that quotes from it? CC BY 4.0. Credit Andhra Record, link back, done.
  • Translating a report into Telugu, Tamil, Hindi, or any other language? CC BY 4.0. Go ahead; email the editor if you'd like the translation linked from the original.
  • Republishing a full report on your news site? CC BY 4.0. Keep the byline. Add "Originally published in Andhra Record at [URL]" at the top or bottom. No permission needed.
  • Using the raw surname-to-caste corpus in your own classifier? ODbL. Credit the source, and if you publish your extended corpus publicly, release it under ODbL too.
  • Forking the Python scrapers to build a similar tool for another state? MIT. Keep the MIT header comment. No other obligations.
  • Citing a statistic in your academic paper? CC BY 4.0 for the statement, ODbL for the underlying dataset if you reuse it directly. Citation format below.
  • Using our work in a paid course, a commercial report, or a book? All three licences permit commercial use. Credit accordingly.

Attribution format

For article text (CC BY 4.0)

When republishing a full article or significant passages, credit as:

By Sahit Koganti / Andhra Record — licensed under CC BY 4.0. Original: https://sahitkogs.github.io/AndhraRecord/[path]

For datasets (ODbL 1.0)

When reusing data:

Contains data from Andhra Record, licensed under ODbL 1.0. Source: https://sahitkogs.github.io/AndhraRecord/

For code (MIT)

The MIT header already lives at the top of every source file. Copy it verbatim when you fork.

For academic citation (APA)

Koganti, S. (2026). [Report title]. Andhra Record. Retrieved from https://sahitkogs.github.io/AndhraRecord/

What is NOT open-licensed

  • Embedded third-party content (quoted text from government documents, statistics from official releases, images from external sources) retains its original rights and you must comply with those separately.
  • The name "Andhra Record", the masthead typography, and any visual identity marks are not trademarked but cannot be used in ways that imply endorsement of your reuse by the publication.
  • Unmasked personal data is not published at all and is not available under any licence. Aggregate and masked data is published under ODbL.

Commercial use

All three licences permit commercial use — without permission, without a fee, without a contract. If your organization requires a bespoke data-sharing agreement for procurement, legal, or warranty reasons, contact the editor and a commercial licence can be arranged for a fee that funds the newsroom. Standard open-licence use is always free.

Why we chose these three

  • CC BY 4.0 over CC BY-NC. Non-commercial clauses are ambiguous in practice (what counts as "commercial"?) and chilling to legitimate republishers who are not sure if they qualify. Maximum reach matters more than enforcement against hypothetical bad actors.
  • ODbL over CC BY for data. Creative Commons licences do not cleanly handle sui-generis database rights, which exist in EU law and (increasingly) in national law. ODbL was designed for databases specifically. It is what OpenStreetMap uses.
  • MIT over GPL for code. GPL's copyleft obligation would force downstream users to open-source their own code, which raises the adoption barrier for other journalists and researchers who want to use our pipelines. MIT's permissiveness is the right default for infrastructure code.
