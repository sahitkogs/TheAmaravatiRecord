EDITORIAL STANDARDS

Editorial Policy

How this newsroom decides what to publish, how to source it, and how to stand behind it.

LAST UPDATED: APRIL 10, 2026  |  VERSION 1.0

The Amaravati Record is a one-desk independent newsroom. It has no managing editor distinct from its author, no advertiser, no board, and no party affiliation. In place of institutional checks, it offers documented rules, open pipelines, and a public corrections ledger. This page states the rules.

1. Independence

The publisher accepts no payment, gift, favour, or in-kind consideration from any political party, government body, corporation, NGO, or individual in exchange for coverage. No story on this site has been commissioned, paid for, suggested, or reviewed by any outside party prior to publication. The publisher will disclose in the byline of any report the existence of any prior relationship with a source or subject that a reasonable reader might view as relevant.

The publisher is a private Indian citizen and reserves the right to hold personal political views, vote, and speak in a personal capacity — while keeping those views out of published reports. Where the subject of a report involves a party or politician the publisher has previously expressed an opinion about, that prior opinion will be disclosed at the top of the relevant report.

2. Sourcing standards

Preference: public data

Every investigation on this site begins, wherever possible, with a public data source — a government portal, a published roll, a ministry release, a court order, or a declared public record. The primary source for the land-pooling investigations is the APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme portal.

Named sources

Named sources are preferred. Where a source is named, the reader can evaluate their credibility independently. Quotes from named sources are published verbatim, with punctuation and grammar unchanged except for spelling corrections or bracketed clarifications.

Anonymous sources

Anonymous sourcing is permitted only when: (a) the information is of clear public interest, (b) it cannot reasonably be obtained on the record, (c) the identity of the source has been verified by the publisher, and (d) the source has a credible reason to fear retaliation. The publisher's editorial discretion over whether these conditions are met is final.

When anonymous sources are used, the report will state the category of source (for example, "a senior APCRDA official familiar with the file") and the reason for anonymity. The publisher maintains records of anonymous sources' identities in encrypted form and will not disclose them to any party, including Indian courts, except upon direct court order after exhausting all appellate remedies.

Secondary sources

Secondary sources — academic papers, press reports, community compilations — are cited with full attribution. Where a secondary source is disputed, this is noted in the text and the dispute is summarized.

3. Verification

Every numerical claim in a published report is traceable to a specific line of the underlying dataset, and every dataset is published alongside the report (or is a public dataset linked from the report). A reader with basic scripting skills should be able to re-derive every headline number by running the published pipeline against the published data.

Qualitative claims — particularly about individuals, institutions, or communities — are verified against at least two independent sources before publication. When that threshold cannot be met, the claim is either dropped or explicitly marked as unverified in the text.

4. Privacy and PII masking

Farmer given names and plot identifiers are masked in every public report. Aggregate statistics by community, village, and plot category are unaffected by the masking. The unmasked underlying data is retained privately and is not shared, sold, or distributed.

This policy applies to every report from the founding edition onward. Earlier drafts that predate the masking policy have been re-published with masked content; their prior versions have been purged from the public repository.

5. Classification and community attribution

When reports attribute plots or names to a caste community, the attribution is based on the surname directory and per-name classifier described in the Methodology. The classification is an approximation, not a census. Individual attributions may be wrong at the margins where surnames are ambiguous. Aggregate statistics at the community level are robust because individual-level errors tend to average out; the reports quantify and disclose residual error rates.

The publication does not endorse the idea that a person's surname determines their character, politics, or worthiness of a land allocation. The reports describe a pattern in public data; interpretation of what that pattern means belongs to readers, scholars, and policy-makers.

6. Conflict of interest

The publisher has no ownership stake in any land allocated under the Amaravati Land Pooling Scheme, is not employed by APCRDA or the Government of Andhra Pradesh, and has no contractual relationship with any political party. Family relationships with any party, including distant ones, that a reasonable reader might view as relevant will be disclosed in the byline of any affected report.

When a report touches on the publisher's own extended surname community, this fact is disclosed at the top of the report.

7. Use of artificial intelligence

The publication uses large language models in specific, disclosed roles — principally the per-name caste classifier based on Google Gemini 2.5 Flash. Model outputs are always cross-checked against a hand-curated ground-truth surname corpus, and the disagreement rate is reported in the dashboard's methodology tab. No model output is published as fact without human review.

Prose — the articles, investigation narratives, and editorial commentary — is written by the publisher personally, sometimes with model assistance for drafting and line editing. Any report in which the core reasoning, investigation, or analysis was performed principally by a model rather than the publisher will be labelled as such. To date no such report has been published.

8. Right of reply

Any individual, organization, or government body named in a published report is entitled to a right of reply. Replies should be emailed to sahit.koganti@gmail.com with the subject line "Right of reply: [report title]". The publisher will review in good faith within 14 days and either (a) publish the reply in full alongside the original report, (b) issue a correction if the reply demonstrates an error of fact, or (c) decline to publish with a stated reason.

A declined right of reply may be submitted to the corrections ledger as a formal complaint, which will itself be published.

9. Corrections

Every correction is logged in the public Corrections Ledger with the date, the affected report, the original text, the corrected text, and the reason. Silent edits — corrections made without a public record — are forbidden except for typographical fixes that do not change meaning.

10. Source protection

The publisher considers source protection a professional duty that supersedes any editorial convenience. Sources who are promised anonymity will be protected against disclosure to any private party and will be disclosed to a court only upon direct order after exhausting all appellate remedies. Encrypted tip channels are documented on the Contact page.

11. Amendments

This policy may be updated as the publication matures. Material changes are logged at the top of this page and announced on the home page for seven days.
