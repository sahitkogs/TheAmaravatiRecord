Nearly Six in Ten Amaravati Plots Are Held by a Single Community

A record-level analysis of 47,993 plots allocated under the APCRDA Land Pooling Scheme shows Kamma beneficiaries at 57.4% of the roll — with Kapu (13.0%) and Reddy (5.0%) trailing well behind — across 26 villages in the Krishna–Guntur corridor.

BY THE AMARAVATI RECORD NEWSROOM  |  APRIL 2026  |  DATA INVESTIGATION

SHARE OF ALLOCATED PLOTS
0% KAMMA · 57.4% 100%
The figure is derived from a deduplicated record of 47,993 unique plots, cleaned from 95,645 raw entries scraped from the gis.apcrda.org portal. Each beneficiary name was classified using a combined surname–corpus and per-name Gemini method, yielding a 99.6% classification rate.

Of the tens of thousands of farmers who surrendered land to the Andhra Pradesh Capital Region Development Authority under the Land Pooling Scheme, a majority of the reconstituted plots now trace back to a single community. The finding emerges from an analysis conducted by this publication's newsroom across the full beneficiary roll of twenty-six villages in the Krishna–Guntur capital corridor — a dataset running to forty-seven thousand nine hundred and ninety-three unique plots after deduplication.

“This is not a narrative assertion. It is what the public beneficiary roll itself shows — line by line, village by village — once every name is classified.”

The classification pipeline combines a five thousand–surname ground truth corpus assembled from nineteen independent sources with a per-name pass through a large language model for the residual cases. The two methods agree on the bulk of the dataset and disagree only at the margins, where first names or regionally ambiguous surnames break the lookup.

The full dispatch — including village-level breakdowns, a surname explorer, and the methodological note — is available in the interactive dashboard and the companion broadsheet edition. Both are released under public-interest terms.

FROM THE EDITOR

Why This Record, and Why Now

A READER WRITES — APRIL 2026:

“There is already plenty of reporting on Amaravati in the mainstream press. What is this publication trying to add?”

FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK:

What the mainstream press covers well is the politics — who said what, which party is for or against the capital this week. What gets covered less is the underlying record: who owns the land, which sites are actually under construction, what the beneficiary rolls say once you read every single name on them. This publication exists to do the slow, record-level work that rarely fits into a daily news cycle. Every report we publish is built from public data and released with the pipeline intact, so a reader can check our arithmetic.

A READER WRITES — APRIL 2026:

“Is the caste analysis meant to be an accusation?”

FROM THE EDITOR'S DESK:

No. It is a descriptive statistic extracted from a public roll of beneficiaries. The reader is invited to draw their own conclusions about what a 57.4% concentration means, and whether that pattern is consistent with the stated aims of the Land Pooling Scheme. The publication's job is to surface the number; interpretation belongs to readers, scholars, and policy-makers.
